Arguments

This section presents key pro‑theory arguments supporting the Ancient Astronaut hypothesis alongside steel‑manned critiques and succinct rebuttals. Each argument is scored for strength based on available evidence.

Pro‑Theory Arguments

  • Megalithic Engineering: The weight, precision and transportation of stones at sites like Baalbek and Giza suggest knowledge or technology beyond known Bronze‑Age capabilities. Strength: Moderate.
  • Global Myth Similarities: Many cultures have myths of sky‑gods, flying chariots and gods descending from the heavens, implying a common source. Strength: Open.
  • Advanced Astronomy: Alignments of monuments to solstices, equinoxes and star systems indicate sophisticated astronomical understanding that may have been taught. Strength: Moderate.
  • Out‑of‑Place Artefacts (OOPArts): Objects like the Antikythera mechanism and intricate metalwork are cited as evidence of lost or alien technology. Strength: Strong.
  • Artistic Depictions: Reliefs such as the Dendera “light” and Abydos inscriptions are claimed to show modern devices. Strength: Open.
  • Ancient Texts: Descriptions of flying machines (vimanas) and celestial battles may reflect encounters with advanced craft. Strength: Open.

Critiques

  • Human Ingenuity: Archaeological evidence shows that ancient societies possessed the ingenuity, social organisation and time to accomplish complex projects without external help.
  • Lack of Physical Evidence: No artefacts of extraterrestrial origin have been recovered; claims rely on interpretation of existing human artefacts.
  • Pareidolia and Misinterpretation: Perceiving modern machines in ancient art is a cognitive bias; textual passages are taken out of mythological context.
